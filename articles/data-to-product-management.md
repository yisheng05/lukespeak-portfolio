My career didn't begin with a formal Product Management title; it began with data, operations, and the urgent need to solve immediate business bottlenecks. This transition—from executing queries to executing product strategy—was completely organic.

## The Context: A Startup Scaling Fast

During my time at **Janio Asia**, the operations team faced significant scaling pains. The operational velocity required to manage thousands of cross-border eCommerce shipments meant that manual tracking and anomaly detection were simply unfeasible. 

The primary bottleneck manifested as "delivery alerts." Key shipments would hit snags, and without real-time visibility, resolving them required immense manual effort. 

## The Solution: A Dashboard Built on Necessity

Before building "features," I focused on value. I needed to automate delivery bottleneck alerts to eliminate the need for part-time staff who would have otherwise conducted manual monitoring and reporting.

Working with open-source tools to maintain a lean stack, I turned to:
- **Superset:** For rapid dashboard prototyping and visualization.
- **Python / Airflow:** For reliable data orchestration and alert generation.

### The True Impact

The direct outcome was saving **50 operations man-days a year**. That metric was crucial for our margins. 

But the secondary—and arguably more important—outcome was cultural. By demonstrating the power of automated systems over manual interventions, I fostered a culture where stakeholders began asking, "Can we automate this?" rather than "Who can we hire to do this manually?"

## The Shift to Product Mindset

This project was a turning point. It evolved my perspective from **Data Analyst** (interpreting what the data says) to **Product Manager** (building systems that alter business outcomes based on what the data says). 

I wasn't just building a dashboard; I was managing the lifecycle of an internal data product. I gathered requirements from the ops team, balanced the technical debt of open-source deployment, iteratively shipped improvements, and ultimately "sunsetted" legacy manual processes. 

It taught me that the best product strategies are rarely born in a boardroom; they are forged by solving painful operational realities on the ground floor.
